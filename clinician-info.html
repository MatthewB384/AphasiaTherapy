Information for Clinicians About the website: The Phono-factory is a free website containing therapy materials for adults with aphasia and/or apraxia of speech. It was developed as a HSC school project by Matthew Banney, as part of an assignment in coding/website development. Clinical content was supplied by the speech pathology department of the John Hunter Hospital. When users have completed therapy tasks from the website, a record of their scores will be stored locally, so that they (or their clinician) can track their performance. Users can see only the data for tasks that were completed on the device they used to access the therapy. About the therapy: Phonological Components Analysis (PCA) Originally the work of Leonard, Rochon & Laird (2008), PCA is a phonologically based treatment for naming deficits in aphasia. It was modelled after the semantic feature analysis (SFA) approach (Boyle & Coelho, 1995) and has been demonstrated to be an effective approach to improving word retrieval for treated and untreated word vocabulary (Leonard et al, 2008; van Hees et al, 2013; Leonard et al, 2015). It promotes activation of the phonological level of the lexical system from the semantic level. Preliminary results suggest that PCA may be more beneficial than SFA for patients who have deficits in the semantic system (van Hees et al, 2013), as well as being useful for patients with post-semantic impairments (van Hees et al, 2013). The original therapy requires the patient to name a picture (if they can) and then identify phonological components related to the target item (rhymes with…, first sound, first sound associates, final sound, number of syllables). For the purpose of creating the interactive therapy materials on this website, which can be completed independently by users, an adapted version of the therapy has been presented in this website, maintaining the principles of active engagement of the user with the therapy stimulus and deeper processing of the stimulus materials (Leonard et al, 2015). Implicit Phoneme Manipulation (IPM) With some intriguing similarities to PCA, implicit phoneme manipulation is a therapy that requires phoneme manipulation (but not verbal production), and is hypothesized to activate motor planning within the cortex. The developers of the therapy (Davis, Farias & Baynes, 2009) proposed that repeated implicit (non-verbal) practice of retrieving and internally monitoring sounds may improve the efficiency of transition between phonological plans and the initial phase of motor planning. They proposed that the intervention could therefore target apraxia of speech and improve speech production without the need for overt (verbal) practice. The therapy used in their preliminary studies required implicit manipulation of phonemes through rhyming, deletion, and alliteration tasks (Davis et al, 2009), and blending (Farias et al, 2013), and was delivered via a computer program. Participants selected picture-based responses on the screen and were discouraged from verbally attempting the target words. Though the data available on this approach is insufficient in scale to be compelling, preliminary results supported the hypothesis that implicit phoneme manipulation improves accuracy of word production in subjects with apraxia of speech. The therapy presented on this website is based on the materials described by the researchers, with adaptations. Given the overlap between IPM and PCA activities, the IPM materials have theoretical potential to be beneficial to people with aphasic word retrieval difficulties.
